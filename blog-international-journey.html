From Riyadh to Brackett Field: An International Student's Training Journey

February 10, 2025 · By Khalid A., Co-Founder & CFI · 7 min read
← Back to Blog

When Youssef landed at LAX in the fall of 2023, he had a dream, a student visa, and a thick folder of TSA paperwork. He had never been to California before. He'd researched flight schools across the United States for months and chose PTC specifically because of our experience with international students and our Part 141 program structure. Less than 14 months later, he held his FAA Commercial Pilot Certificate. This is his story — and a practical guide for anyone considering the same path.

Why International Students Choose the US for Flight Training

The United States has the world's largest and most complex aviation training environment. The combination of excellent weather (especially in Southern California), high-quality aircraft, experienced instructors, and the FAA's globally recognized certification system makes American flight schools the top choice for aspiring pilots from across the Middle East, Asia, Africa, Latin America, and Europe.

An FAA Commercial Pilot License with an Instrument Rating is respected worldwide and serves as a foundation for licensing conversions in most countries. Many international students who train in the US go on to fly for airlines in their home countries or in the Gulf, Asia-Pacific, or European markets.

For Youssef, the goal was clear: earn his CPL and instrument rating in the US, then return to Saudi Arabia to pursue a career with a regional carrier. The US certification pathway was the most direct route to that goal.

Step 1: The TSA/FTSP Process

This is the part that intimidates most international students — and understandably so. The TSA's Flight Training Security Program (FTSP) requires non-US citizens to submit to a background check and receive TSA approval before beginning flight training. Here's a simplified overview of the process:

  1. Create an account at the TSA FTSP website (flightschoolcandidates.gov)
  2. Submit your application with valid passport, visa, and personal information
  3. Pay the $130 processing fee
  4. Provide your fingerprints at a TSA-designated location or through a 3rd party provider
  5. Wait for approval — typically 5–30 business days depending on your country of origin
  6. Receive your Candidate ID number, which you provide to the flight school

At PTC, we've helped dozens of students navigate this process and we walk you through it step by step. Our international admissions team stays current with TSA requirements and can answer your specific questions. Don't let the paperwork intimidate you — it's manageable with the right guidance.

Important: You cannot log any flight training time until TSA approval is confirmed. Plan accordingly. Most students use the waiting period to study for their FAA written exams, get their medical certificate, and get settled in the area.

Step 2: Getting Settled in La Verne

Youssef found an apartment in Pomona about 10 minutes from the airport. La Verne and the surrounding Pomona Valley area have a large international community, solid food options including halal restaurants, and affordable housing compared to most of LA County. Several of our international students over the years have found the area to be a surprisingly comfortable home base for their training period.

A used car is essentially a necessity — Southern California's transit options are limited, and the early-morning or late-afternoon flight windows that instructors often prefer mean you need reliable transportation. Budget $3,000–$6,000 for a dependable used vehicle.

Step 3: Training Begins

Once TSA approval came through, Youssef started training with an intensity that set him apart. He was flying 4–5 days a week, spending evenings in our ground school, and filling every free hour with studying for his FAA knowledge exams.

His timeline looked like this:

  • Month 1–3: Private Pilot ground school, flying 3–4x/week, PPL written exam
  • Month 4: PPL practical (checkride) — passed first attempt
  • Month 5–7: Instrument Rating training, IR written exam
  • Month 8: Instrument Rating checkride — passed first attempt
  • Month 9–13: Commercial Pilot training (Part 141 syllabus), CPL written exam
  • Month 14: Commercial checkride — passed first attempt

Three certificates, three passed checkrides, zero failures. That's not luck — it's what structured training and dedicated study produces.

What Youssef Says About His Experience

"Coming to California alone was scary at first. But from the first week at PTC, I felt like I was part of a team. My instructors knew exactly where I was in the syllabus, what I needed to work on, and how to push me without overwhelming me. The structure of the Part 141 program meant I always knew what was coming next. And flying in Southern California — over the mountains, near LA airspace — gave me real experience that other pilots don't have."

He also had practical advice for prospective international students: "Start your TSA paperwork as early as possible. Study English aviation vocabulary before you arrive. And don't be afraid to ask questions — the instructors at PTC are patient and they want you to succeed."
