Why Your Instrument Rating Is the Most Important Certificate You'll Ever Earn

January 8, 2025 · By Kareem M., Chief Flight Instructor · 8 min read
← Back to Blog

Ask any experienced pilot what single training achievement made the biggest difference in their flying, and the answer is almost always the same: the instrument rating. Not the commercial certificate. Not the multi-engine rating. The instrument rating. It changes not just what you can do in an airplane, but fundamentally how you think as a pilot. If you're serious about flying — whether for a career or as a lifelong pursuit — earning your IFR rating should be your first goal after getting your Private.

What the Instrument Rating Actually Is

An Instrument Rating (IR) allows you to fly in Instrument Meteorological Conditions (IMC) — weather that's below Visual Flight Rules (VFR) minimums. That means flying through clouds, in low visibility, at night in overcast conditions, and in weather that would ground a VFR-only pilot. You file an IFR flight plan, receive ATC clearances, and navigate the entire National Airspace System using instruments and procedures.

To earn the rating, the FAA requires at minimum (under Part 61):

  • 50 hours of cross-country PIC time
  • 40 hours of actual or simulated instrument time
  • 15 of those instrument hours with an authorized instructor (CFII)
  • Passing the Instrument Rating Aeronautical Knowledge Test (written exam)
  • Passing an Instrument Rating Practical Test (checkride) with an FAA Examiner

Under Part 141 — which is how we structure it at PTC — the minimums are lower: 35 hours instrument time. See our programs page for full details.

The Safety Case Is Overwhelming

VFR-into-IMC (a VFR pilot accidentally flying into clouds or low visibility) is one of the most deadly accident categories in general aviation. The statistics are brutal: when VFR pilots enter IMC, the accident rate is extremely high and the outcomes are almost always fatal. The reason is spatial disorientation — without visual references, the human body's vestibular system gives false sensations of attitude that can lead a pilot to put an aircraft into a fatal spiral within minutes.

Instrument training teaches you to disregard what your body feels and trust your instruments. That skill doesn't just help you when you're in the clouds on an IFR clearance — it makes you a vastly safer pilot in every flight you take. You recognize the conditions that can get you into trouble, you have better situational awareness, and your basic instrument scan becomes second nature.

The hard truth: A significant percentage of fatal general aviation accidents involve weather as a factor. Many of those accidents involved VFR pilots. Earning your instrument rating is one of the single most impactful safety investments you can make as a pilot. No other training comes close in terms of risk reduction.

What Instrument Training Teaches You

The instrument rating curriculum covers a broad and deep set of skills that professional pilots use every single day:

Aircraft Systems and Navigation

  • Reading and interpreting all primary flight instruments (attitude, altimeter, airspeed, VSI, heading indicator, turn coordinator)
  • VOR, ILS, GPS, and RNAV navigation systems
  • Intercepting and tracking radials, localizers, and glideslopes
  • Holding patterns — the pilot's equivalent of circling in a traffic jam

Charts and Procedures

  • Reading and flying Standard Instrument Departures (SIDs) and Standard Terminal Arrivals (STARs)
  • Precision approaches (ILS) and non-precision approaches (VOR, RNAV/LNAV)
  • Minimums, decision altitude, missed approach procedures
  • Alternate airport requirements and IFR flight planning

ATC Communication

IFR flying puts you in constant contact with ATC. You'll receive clearances, amendments, vectors, and altitude assignments — often in rapid succession. Your radio communication skills will leap forward dramatically during instrument training. The confidence and clarity pilots develop during IFR training is immediately noticeable.

The Career Argument

If you're on a career track, there's no optional discussion here. Every professional flying job requires an instrument rating — no exceptions. Airlines, charter, corporate, cargo, instructing — all require it. Earning it early, during your structured commercial training at a school like PTC, means you do it efficiently and it feeds directly into your commercial certificate requirements.

More importantly, the quality of your instrument flying is something airline recruiters can actually evaluate. How you handle an ILS approach or a hold during a checkride tells an experienced evaluator a great deal about your airmanship. Students who have genuinely excellent instrument skills stand out.

How We Teach the Instrument Rating at PTC

Our instrument rating training at PTC is fully integrated into our Part 141 commercial track. We use our fleet of Cessna 172s equipped with Garmin glass cockpits to teach both traditional and modern navigation. Our CFII instructors are active IFR pilots who fly the same procedures they teach you. You'll fly simulated instrument approaches in Southern California's real ATC environment — one of the most complex in the world — and that experience is genuinely valuable preparation for a professional career.
