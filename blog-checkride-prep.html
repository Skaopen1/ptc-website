How to Ace Your FAA Checkride: A PTC Instructor's Complete Guide

March 5, 2025 · By Khalid A., Co-Founder & CFI · 10 min read
← Back to Blog

The FAA practical test — what every pilot calls the "checkride" — is the final exam of your flight training. It's also one of the most anxiety-inducing experiences in a student pilot's journey. After years of working with students through their Private Pilot, Instrument, and Commercial checkrides, I can tell you this: the students who pass on their first attempt aren't necessarily the most naturally talented pilots. They're the most prepared ones. This guide is everything our instructors at PTC tell students in the weeks before their test.

Understanding What the Checkride Actually Is

The FAA practical test has two parts: an oral examination and a flight portion. Both are conducted by a Designated Pilot Examiner (DPE) — an FAA-authorized private examiner who has been granted the authority to test and certify pilots on the FAA's behalf. The DPE is not your adversary. Their job is to evaluate whether you meet the standard, and if you do, they want to pass you.

The standard is found in the Airman Certification Standards (ACS) — the document that replaced the old Practical Test Standards (PTS). The ACS defines exactly what you'll be tested on, what knowledge is expected, and what performance standards you must meet. It's publicly available and you should own and study it. Reading the ACS is not cheating — it's exactly what you're supposed to do.

The Oral Examination: How to Prepare

The oral portion typically lasts 1–2 hours for a Private Pilot test and can go longer for more complex certificates. The examiner will ask questions across the knowledge areas defined in the ACS, including:

  • Regulations (FARs relevant to your certificate level)
  • Weather theory and forecasting products (METARs, TAFs, PIREPs, SIGMETs)
  • Aerodynamics and aircraft systems
  • Navigation
  • Cross-country flight planning (you'll typically be assigned a cross-country to plan)
  • Airspace and airport operations
  • Aeromedical factors

Strategies that work:

  • Study from the ACS, not just your textbook. The ACS defines exactly what you'll be asked. If it's not in the ACS, you won't be tested on it. If it IS in the ACS, you should know it cold.
  • Practice explaining concepts out loud. Talking through your knowledge is completely different from reading it silently. Do mock oral exams with your instructor — it builds confidence and reveals gaps in your understanding you didn't know were there.
  • Know your aircraft's POH. Examiners love asking system questions that are specific to your aircraft. Know the fuel system, electrical system, pitot-static system, and performance limitations from the Pilot's Operating Handbook.
  • It's acceptable to say "I don't know, but I know how to find out." No pilot knows everything. An honest acknowledgment of a knowledge gap — followed by a demonstration of how you'd look up the answer — is far better than guessing confidently and being wrong.

The most common oral failure reason: Insufficient knowledge of regulations and airspace. Review FAR Part 61 (pilot certificates), FAR Part 91 (general operating rules), and the airspace classification system until you can explain them in your sleep. These are almost universally tested and are objective pass/fail areas.

The Flight Portion: What the DPE Is Looking For

The flight portion tests your ability to perform the tasks listed in the ACS to the specified standards. For Private Pilot, those standards are practical and achievable — the tolerances for altitude, heading, and airspeed are reasonable, not demanding airline precision. For Commercial, the standards are tighter and the maneuver set is more complex.

What examiners notice immediately:

  • Preflight: Is it thorough or rushed? Do you know what you're looking at and why?
  • Checklist discipline: Are you using checklists as the primary operating tool, or as an afterthought?
  • Traffic scanning: Are you actively looking for other aircraft throughout the flight?
  • Radio communication: Clear, accurate, professional, and appropriate
  • Airspace awareness: Do you know where you are relative to any controlled airspace at all times?

Maneuver Performance Tips

Practice each required maneuver until it's automatic, not just acceptable. The difference between a student who occasionally meets standards and a student who consistently exceeds them is confident, relaxed execution. Tension causes control inputs to be jerky and imprecise. Familiarity produces smooth, coordinated flight.

  • For steep turns: establish your bank angle, then set a power adjustment to maintain altitude. Trust the trim and let the airplane fly.
  • For stalls: fly them to the break, not just the buffet. Examiners want to see full stall recognition and recovery.
  • For emergency landings: establish best glide immediately, pick your field in the first 30 seconds, and run the engine failure checklist. Talk out loud — it reassures the examiner that you're thinking correctly.

The Week Before: Practical Preparation

In the final week before your checkride:

  1. Complete a full "mock checkride" with your instructor — oral and flight, in real checkride conditions
  2. Review the ACS one more time, checking every task and making sure you're confident on the standards
  3. Confirm all your documents are ready: logbook endorsements, knowledge test results, government-issued ID, medical certificate, and the aircraft airworthiness documents (use the ARROW acronym)
  4. Get proper sleep. Fatigue degrades judgment and motor skill performance more than almost any other factor
  5. Eat before the test. Low blood sugar will affect your cognitive performance

If Something Goes Wrong During the Test

If you make a mistake during the flight portion, don't catastrophize. Everyone makes mistakes. What matters is how you respond. Correct the error calmly and professionally, just as you would on any training flight. Examiners are not looking for perfection — they're looking for a pilot who has sound judgment, recognizes errors, and corrects them appropriately.

If you genuinely don't know how to perform a maneuver correctly mid-flight, it's better to say "I need a moment" and compose yourself than to push ahead into a poor-quality performance. Examiners appreciate self-awareness.
