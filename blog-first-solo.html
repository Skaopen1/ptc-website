5 Things Every Student Pilot Should Know Before Their First Solo

February 28, 2025 · By Sarah M., Senior CFI · 6 min read
← Back to Blog

The day of your first solo flight is unlike any other in your aviation journey. You've practiced touch-and-goes dozens of times, your instructor has reviewed everything with you, and then — they step out of the airplane and send you off alone. It's exhilarating, terrifying, and deeply satisfying all at once. After years of training students at PTC, here are the five most important things I tell every student pilot before they fly alone for the first time.

1. Your Instructor Wouldn't Send You If You Weren't Ready

This sounds simple, but it's worth saying clearly: your CFI's job is to keep you safe. We don't send students solo out of convenience or schedule pressure. When your instructor steps out of that airplane, they have watched you demonstrate every skill needed to handle that aircraft safely — takeoffs, landings, traffic pattern procedures, radio communication, and emergency procedures. If they thought there was any doubt, the flight would be postponed.

Nervousness is completely normal and actually healthy — it keeps you sharp. But don't confuse nerves with unreadiness. Trust the training and trust your instructor's judgment. They've seen hundreds of student pilots, and they know when someone is ready.

2. Fly the Pattern Exactly As Practiced

Solo day is not the time to experiment or change anything. Every altitude, every checkpoint, every radio call should be exactly what you've been doing with your instructor in the right seat. The traffic pattern you've practiced is your safety framework — stick to it religiously.

  • Maintain pattern altitude (at Brackett, 2,000 ft MSL for Runway 26L)
  • Make all five required radio calls: taxi, takeoff, crosswind, downwind, final
  • Keep your checklist items in sequence: GUMPS or equivalent before landing
  • Don't rush the pattern — if you're high on final, it's always okay to go around

Deviating from a familiar pattern introduces variables your mind hasn't rehearsed. Keep everything exactly as practiced.

3. A Go-Around Is Always the Right Call

Student pilots sometimes feel pressure to "save" a bad approach and turn it into a salvageable landing. Experienced pilots know that the go-around is one of the most important and underused maneuvers in aviation. If anything feels wrong on final — too high, too fast, a vehicle on the runway, a wind gust — apply full power immediately and go around without hesitation.

Remember this: There is no such thing as a committed landing in a small aircraft. Up until the moment of touchdown, you have the option and the obligation to go around if the situation calls for it. Practicing go-arounds with your instructor before solo is one of the most important prep steps we do at PTC.

4. The Airplane Will Feel Different Without Your Instructor

One thing that surprises almost every student on their first solo: the airplane performs noticeably differently without a second occupant. In a Cessna 172, your instructor might weigh 150–220 lbs. Remove that weight from the right seat and you've meaningfully changed the aircraft's center of gravity, performance, and handling.

Specifically, expect:

  • Faster acceleration on takeoff — the aircraft will be lighter and will climb more aggressively
  • Different pitch trim requirements — you may need to adjust trim more than usual
  • Slightly different sight picture on landing — the nose may appear slightly lower

Your instructor will brief you on these differences beforehand. The important thing is to not be startled by them — just fly the airplane.

5. Celebrate, Then Debrief

Your first solo is a huge achievement — only about 1 in 5 Americans who start flight training actually complete their Private Pilot Certificate. The fact that you've made it to solo puts you in a very special group. Celebrate that milestone fully.

And then, debrief it seriously. What went well? What would you do differently? Were your radio calls confident and clear? Did you maintain your altitudes? The debriefing habit you build now will make you a better pilot throughout your entire career — from student pilot all the way to captain.

At PTC, after a student's first solo, we like to do a proper debrief over the flight, talk through any moments that felt uncertain, and set goals for the next lesson. Training is a continuous feedback loop, and the more honest and specific your self-assessment, the faster you improve.
