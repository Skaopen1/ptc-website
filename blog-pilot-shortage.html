The 2025 Pilot Shortage: Why Right Now Is the Best Time to Earn Your Wings

March 15, 2025 · By Khalid A., Co-Founder & CFI · 8 min read
← Back to Blog

If you've been thinking about becoming a pilot but wondering whether now is the right time, let me put your mind at ease: there has never been a better moment in modern aviation history to start your training. The 2025 pilot shortage is not a news headline — it's a structural, decades-long demographic wave, and it is reshaping what a career in aviation looks like for the next generation of aviators.

Understanding the Scale of the Shortage

Boeing's 2024 Pilot & Technician Outlook projects that the aviation industry will need more than 649,000 new commercial pilots globally over the next 20 years. In North America alone, the demand is forecast at over 180,000 pilots. Airlines are already feeling the pressure. Regional carriers have cut routes not because of a lack of passengers, but because they simply don't have enough captains to fly the planes.

The root cause is straightforward: the post-9/11 aviation slowdown and the 2008 recession caused a significant dip in pilot training enrollment. Those would-be pilots never came. Meanwhile, thousands of captains hired during the 1980s and 1990s boom are hitting the mandatory retirement age of 65 every single year. The math is unforgiving — retirements are outpacing new hires at major carriers, and regionals are bearing the brunt of it.

By the numbers: American Airlines, United, and Delta are each hiring hundreds of pilots per month. First-year regional captain salaries now routinely exceed $150,000 — up from under $50,000 just a decade ago. Major airline captains regularly earn $300,000–$400,000+ annually.

What This Means for You Financially

The compensation landscape has been completely transformed. Here's what the market looks like in 2025:

  • Regional First Officers are now starting at $80,000–$100,000+ per year
  • Regional Captains typically earn $130,000–$200,000
  • Major airline First Officers start at $130,000–$170,000
  • Major airline Captains earn $250,000–$450,000+ depending on aircraft type and seniority
  • Signing bonuses at some regionals now exceed $100,000 in some programs

Beyond base salary, airline pilots receive exceptional benefits: free and reduced-rate travel for themselves and family members, defined-benefit pension plans (at many carriers), and some of the best health insurance packages in any industry. It is a genuinely compelling financial proposition.

The Pathway Has Also Gotten Clearer

Major airlines don't just want you — they're investing in bringing you along. Many carriers have established "flow-through" agreements with regional partners. That means if you get hired at a regional airline, you are automatically guaranteed an interview (and often a conditional hire date) at the affiliated major airline. United's Aviate program, American's Cadet Academy, and Delta's Propel program are all examples of structured pipelines that connect flight school to the right seat of a 787 or A320.

At PTC, we have helped students prepare for and successfully enter these pipelines. Our Part 141 program meets the structured requirements that many of these cadet programs look for when evaluating candidates.

Timeline to the Airlines

For context, here's a realistic timeline for a dedicated student starting from zero:

  1. Private Pilot Certificate (PPL): 3–5 months
  2. Instrument Rating (IR): 3–5 months after PPL
  3. Commercial Pilot Certificate (CPL): 3–5 months after IR
  4. Flight Instructor Certificate (CFI): 1–3 months after CPL
  5. Build hours as a CFI to reach 1,500 hours ATP minimums: 1–2 years
  6. Regional airline hire: As soon as ATP minimums are met

From zero to regional airline hire: approximately 3–4 years of focused effort. That's a remarkably short runway to a six-figure career.

Why Waiting Costs You More Than You Think

Every year you delay is a year of seniority you'll never get back. Airline pay and lifestyle are almost entirely determined by seniority — your place on the list determines your schedule, your base, the aircraft you fly, and ultimately your earnings. Getting in earlier means retiring at the top of the list, with the best schedules, the most pay, and the most options.

There's also the simple reality of opportunity cost. If you could be earning $100,000 a year as a regional FO but you're delaying training by 12 months, that's a very real $100,000 you're leaving on the table. Start the clock now.
